UNIT 01: PRINCIPLES OF CHEMISTRY

1. STATES OF MATTER

1.1 Arrangement of Particles

The arrangement and energy of particles differs in Solids, Liquids, and Gases.

Solid

  • Arrangement: Regular, packed closely together.
  • Movement: Vibrate in fixed positions.
  • Forces: Strong attraction forces keep particles together.
  • Energy: Lowest kinetic energy.

Liquid

  • Arrangement: Random, mostly touching but with small gaps. Less dense than solids.
  • Movement: Move around past each other.
  • Forces: Less attraction than solids.
  • Energy: More kinetic energy than solids, less than gas.

Gas

  • Arrangement: Random, particles are far apart.
  • Movement: Moving rapidly.
  • Forces: No forces of attraction.
  • Energy: Highest kinetic energy.

1.2 Interconversions (Changing States)

1. Solid ↔ Liquid

Melting (Solid to Liquid):

  • When heated, particles vibrate faster.
  • Energy breaks the strong attraction forces.
  • Occurs at the Melting Point.

Freezing (Liquid to Solid):

  • When cooled, particles move slower.
  • Attraction forces hold them in fixed positions.
  • Occurs at the Freezing Point.

2. Liquid ↔ Gas

This can happen in two ways: Boiling or Evaporation.

Feature Boiling Evaporation
Heat Source Liquid is heated strongly Liquid is not heated directly
Visual Bubbles are seen No bubbles
Speed Happens quickly Happens slowly
Temperature Fixed temperature (Boiling Point) Can occur at any temperature

3. Solid ↔ Gas (Sublimation)

Conversion directly from solid to gas (Sublimation) or gas to solid (Deposition/De-sublimation).

Example: Carbon Dioxide ($CO_2$)

  • Solid $CO_2$ is called Dry Ice (White solid).
  • At ordinary pressure, it turns directly into gas.
  • Reaction: $CO_2(s) \rightarrow CO_2(g)$
  • Note: $CO_2$ gas is invisible. The "white cloud" seen is water vapour from the air condensing because the $CO_2$ is so cold.

1.3 Physical State at Temperature

Example: Water

  • Melting Point: 0°C (Below 0°C = Solid Ice).
  • Boiling Point: 100°C (Above 100°C = Gas/Steam).
  • Between 0°C and 100°C = Liquid Water.

1.4 Diffusion

Definition: The movement of particles from an area of high concentration to an area of low concentration.

Experiment 1: Diffusion in Gases (Bromine)

  • Setup: A jar of air (colorless) is placed on top of a jar of Bromine gas (Brown).
  • Observation: The brown color slowly spreads upwards until both jars are uniformly brown.
  • Explanation: Particles move randomly and bounce off air particles, eventually mixing completely.

Experiment 2: Reaction of Ammonia and Hydrogen Chloride

Reaction: $NH_3 (gas) + HCl (gas) \rightarrow NH_4Cl (white\ solid)$

  • Cotton wool soaked in Ammonia ($NH_3$) is placed at one end of a tube.
  • Cotton wool soaked in Hydrochloric Acid ($HCl$) is placed at the other end.
  • Observation: A white ring of Ammonium Chloride forms.
  • Location: The ring forms closer to the $HCl$ end.
  • Conclusion: Ammonia particles are lighter than HCl particles, so they diffuse (travel) faster.

Experiment 3: Diffusion in Liquids & Dilution

Observation: Diffusion in liquids is much slower than in gases because liquid particles are closer together and collide more often.

Potassium Manganate(VII) Dilution
  • A tiny crystal (0.01g) creates a deep purple solution in water.
  • Even after diluting 10,000 times, the solution remains pink/purple.
  • Conclusion: Particles (atoms/molecules) must be extremely small. A single crystal contains millions of particles to be able to spread out that much.

1.5 Solubility

Key Terms:

  • Solute: The solid that dissolves (e.g., Salt).
  • Solvent: The liquid it dissolves in (e.g., Water).
  • Solution: The mixture formed (e.g., Saltwater).
  • Saturated Solution: A solution where no more solute can dissolve at that temperature.

Solubility Curve

Solubility is measured as the grams of solute per 100g of solvent.

Calculation Example: Crystallization

Scenario:

  • At 90°C, the solubility of $KNO_3$ is 200g per 100g water.
  • At 30°C, the solubility of $KNO_3$ is 50g per 100g water.

Question: If a saturated solution is cooled from 90°C to 30°C, how much solid crystallizes?

Answer: $200g - 50g = \mathbf{150g}$ of crystals formed.
